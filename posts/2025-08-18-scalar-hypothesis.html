📍 Hypothesis Overview

The classic model of neural communication emphasizes chemical synapses, ion potentials, and Hebbian firing. But what if sub-threshold synchrony — coherence without spiking — is mediated by a nonlocal field phenomenon?

This post considers the possibility of scalar field coupling as a substrate for global brain-state modulation, particularly across microcircuits and long-range coherence domains (e.g. hippocampus ↔ prefrontal cortex).

📐 Scalar Field Recap

Scalar fields assign a single value to every point in space — unlike vector fields which define direction + magnitude. They can represent:

  • Temperature gradients
  • Charge density maps
  • Potential fields in physics (e.g. gravitational, electric)

In nonlinear media, scalar fields may interact to form standing waves or nonlinear envelopes that modulate phase behavior of nearby oscillators — a potential candidate for cross-synaptic coherence tuning.

🔬 Local vs Nonlocal Coupling

Typical neural interactions are bound by axonal delay and chemical diffusion time. Scalar coupling, if it exists, could exhibit:

  • Zero-lag phase alignment (as seen in EEG coherence studies)
  • Distance-invariant effects
  • Stability under perturbation (nonlinear entrainment)

This suggests a mechanism that operates beyond direct wiring, modulating dynamics across embedded fields — a kind of neural scalar bus.

🧬 Potential Bio-Physical Substrates

  • Microtubules → proposed as coherent EM oscillators (Hameroff & Penrose)
  • Axonal sheaths → capable of guiding slow EM waveforms
  • Interstitial water → dynamic dielectric matrix, possibly scalar-responsive
  • Glial networks → tuned to subthreshold ionic shifts and field flux

None of this requires breaking known physics — only acknowledging that field gradients and oscillators can couple below synaptic thresholds.
